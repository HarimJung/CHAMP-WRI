How Morocco Connects Cities to National Climate Goals

From Chefchaouen to Rabat — multilevel partnerships in action

Scroll

When Morocco endorsed CHAMP at COP28 in December 2023, the commitment carried weight. The country was already navigating one of the most ambitious energy transitions in North Africa — a national target to cut emissions by 45.5% by 2030. But national targets mean little without local implementation. Morocco's challenge was connecting its cities to its climate plans.

That connection is now happening. In June 2025, over 100 leaders from national and local governments, international agencies, and civil society gathered in Rabat for the UrbanShift Morocco Multilevel Governance Dialogue. The question on the table: how can Morocco transform its resources, waste, and energy into engines of resilient growth?

The Local Push

"The circular economy is not only an environmental imperative — it is an opportunity for inclusive and sustainable economic transformation." That was the message from Minister Leila Benali, Morocco's Minister of Energy Transition and Sustainable Development, as she opened the Rabat dialogue.

In Chefchaouen, a city of 45,000 known worldwide for its blue-washed streets, the municipal council had already committed to local climate action. But without structured alignment to national targets, local efforts risked fragmentation. The CHAMP framework changed that — creating a formal consultation channel between cities and the national government's NDC process.

"True change requires systemic innovation — rethinking how resources are used, shared, and valued."

— Paul Currie, ICLEI Africa, at the Rabat Dialogue

Three Steps in Practice

01

Consult

Morocco's Ministry of Energy Transition held regional consultations with municipal councils across the country, integrating local perspectives into the national NDC update. The UrbanShift Dialogue in Rabat was a direct product of this process.

02

Collaborate

For the first time, city-level data on waste, water, and energy was being collected to inform national reporting. International partners including UNDP, UNIDO, and ICLEI provided technical support.

03

Implement

The dialogue's findings will feed directly into Morocco's circular economy roadmap, with a follow-up national dialogue scheduled to examine how recommendations have been integrated into local implementation.

What This Means for CHAMP

Morocco's experience is exactly what CHAMP was designed to produce: a national government that doesn't just set climate targets, but builds the multilevel infrastructure to deliver them. When Chefchaouen's waste management plans align with Rabat's national strategy, climate action stops being a document and starts being a system.

As CHAMP grows beyond 78 endorsers heading into COP31, stories like Morocco's become the evidence base for the coalition's core argument — that national climate plans work better when cities help write them.
